# Car Guy Simulator - Story Department Notes
### The "Taylor Sheridan pass": what he would touch, deepen, and develop

## The one-line diagnosis

The game already has Sheridan's STRUCTURE: seasons that end in winter, work as
ritual, a marriage with real cost, quiet weeks that matter, an ending about the
next tarp instead of the trophy. What it's missing is his first commandment:
everything comes from somewhere. The truck has no history, the town has no
name, and the two best characters in the script are loaded guns nobody has
fired. This pass wouldn't add plot. It would add weight.

## 1. What he'd keep and never touch

- The quiet weeks. "Some weeks you wrench. Some weeks you stand in the garage
  with a coffee and just look at her." The family cruise as default path is
  the most Sheridan mechanic in the game.
- The wife rebuild. "You should have asked me. I would have said yes. That's
  what kills me." The porch light going off is the right punishment: silence.
- The finale trilogy: triumph, empty garage, next tarp. End on the cost.
- The ring's June date pinned to the fridge with a hot-rod magnet.
- The bedside box: tools that arrive with rust and history, not a receipt.

## 2. The two loaded guns to fire immediately

DALE'S FOUR WORDS. "Oh, you're the one." Never pays off. Fix: Dale knew this
truck. At trust tier three, mid-estimate: "You know I did the brakes on this
truck in '89. Fella that owned her never finished anything in his life. Kept
the tarp on. You gonna be different?" The barn find gets a ghost; Dale becomes
the keeper of the town's memory; the player is answering a question somebody
else already failed.

THE UNCLE. The hand-me-down toolbox says he "is done chasing projects." A
whole man in six words: the one who taught you, who quit. Develop: he shows up
once, at your first Regional, stands at the back, leaves before trophies. DM
that night: "Saw the truck run. Your welds are cleaner than mine ever were.
Don't tell your aunt I drove out." New credits line at the ceremony: "FOR THE
MAN WHO QUIT SO YOU'D KNOW WHAT IT COSTS."

## 3. Character notes

BUCK: a heel with no wound. Sheridan antagonists are right about something and
bleeding somewhere. After your first Nationals win, one DM past the cope: "my
old man ran this scene twenty years. never once watched me drive. anyway.
car's right. won't say it twice." Optional later knife: Buck offers to buy
your build at full price.

THE WIFE: has a voice now; give her a want. Her car gets one sentence of
history before you can sell it: "She learned to drive stick in that car. Her
dad taught her in a church parking lot." And once, unprompted, she picks a
respray color: "paint it the blue. trust me." Whatever you do, she never
mentions it again.

DALE: the game's Lloyd. Add one mortality beat at max trust: "Shop's mine
another five years, maybe. Kid in Fresno wants to buy the lot for storage
units. Anyway. Torque converter's done Thursday."

THE KIDS: mentioned, never present. One beat per season: the oldest hands you
a wrench. Season one it's the wrong one. Season three it's the right one,
before you ask. The next tarp eventually becomes their tarp.

THE SHARK: leaves the back room once. He shows up at a show in daylight,
leans on your fender with a funnel cake, says "Nice crowd," and leaves.

THE RIVALS: one ally-tier confession DM each. Leo the Ghost's: "i don't post.
i don't talk. you build like the car's the only witness. same reason i do it."

## 4. Place: the biggest structural note

The town has no name. Name it once, in the ticker, never explain it. Put it on
the Annual's masthead. And give the fairground stakes: "County's talking about
selling the fairground lot. This might be the last year of the Regional."
The scene under threat makes every entry fee an act of faith.

## 5. Dialogue pass (line level)

- Ledger and liturgy don't mix: move stat readouts ("engine +7") out of scene
  prose and into toasts/recaps.
- Ration the echoes: one "Never again." per game (keep Tijuana's, cut the
  shark payoff's).
- Emoji stay in DMs (diegetic), out of dramatic scene bodies.
- The Annual's lede should carry one sentence of elegy per season: what the
  year took, not just what it paid.

No new art required; the uncle-at-the-fence would make a strong cutscene
prompt when wanted. The whole table is roughly two pushes of writing.

## Bottom line

The game already knows the secret: this was never about cars. It's about what
a man owes the people who believed in him and the place that made him. The gap
is the difference between knowing it and saying it out loud, once, quietly, at
the right moment.
